The Stylistic Conventions of Deconstructivist Architecture:

  • Embraces fragmentation and unconventional design
  • Rejects symmetry
  • Rejects definite structure
  • Utilizes geometry, sharp edges and mirrored facades.

An Overview On Deconstructivism:

Deconstructivist architecture emerged during the 1980s alongside several other post-modern movements seeking to redefine what architecture could be, much like the modernist movement itself. While the name deconstructivism is sometimes believed to reference the so-called “deconstructed” appearance of deconstructivist architecture, deconstructivists sought to reference Russian constructivism, conceived during WWI, which attempted to break the rules or disrupt classical architectural conventions. What makes deconstructivism unique when compared to other post-modern movements is the desire to confront architectural history and disassemble the canon, whereas post-modernists embraced history and sought to incorporate aspects of older styles. In a sense, deconstructivism is more of a philosophy for design than an architectural style in and of itself, that being said deconstructivist architecture often references philosophy such as post-structuralism rather than other movements, doing so would defy the entire purpose of Deconstructivist theory.
